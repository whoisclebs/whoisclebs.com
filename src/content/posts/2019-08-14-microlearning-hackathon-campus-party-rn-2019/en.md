I'm in my second semester of college and I just left my first hackathon. It still feels strange to write this, because Campus Party RN seemed to compress so much into just a few days: anxiety, code, new people, sleepless nights, and the feeling that I had stepped into a kind of experience that would repeat itself many more times.

The challenge was to create a microlearning solution to improve the training of public servants in Rio Grande do Norte. At the time, I was still getting to know my college classmates, better understanding where I fit in, and trying to turn curiosity into technical repertoire.

In this hackathon, I worked with Marcos, Josinaldo, and Wesley.

## The first all-nighter for code

It was one of the first times I pulled an all-nighter because of code — something I didn't yet know would become frequent in my life.

There's a specific kind of learning that only shows up when the clock is against you. The initial plan almost never survives intact. Bugs appear at bad times. The presentation has to be ready. The backend needs to talk to the frontend. And, in the middle of all that, the team needs to keep functioning.

At that moment, I was discovering this in practice.

## Django, Vue.js 2, and learning

Our solution used Django on the backend and Vue.js 2 on the frontend. For me, it was a very concrete opportunity to learn by doing. It wasn't a tutorial, it wasn't an isolated college exercise — it was a solution that needed to be demonstrated to judges, with a deadline, a narrative, and a purpose.

Beyond the technical side, we used Bloom's Taxonomy to better structure the training material. This was important because the problem wasn't just about delivering content in smaller chunks. The solution needed to think about learning progression, comprehension, and application.

## Third place

We took 3rd place.

In the moment, of course, there's that mix of happiness with the feeling that we could have done more. But looking back at the experience, the outcome was enormous. It was my first hackathon, my first coding all-nighter, one of the first strong experiences of technical collaboration, and one of the first moments where I realized how technology, product, and presentation need to walk together.

Campus Party RN became a starting point. After it, hackathons stopped being a distant idea and became a space where I could learn fast, meet people, and test what I knew under pressure.

A lot of what I do today still carries a bit of that dawn: simplify, divide responsibility, stay focused on the problem, and deliver something that makes sense even when time is short.
