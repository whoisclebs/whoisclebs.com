Almost a year after my first hackathon, here I am again. This time, at the Open Data Hackathon of Campus Party Digital Goiás, right in the middle of the pandemic, with a team where I barely knew anyone — except for Josinaldo Júnior, who had been with me since the first hackathon.

It was a completely different experience. In 2019, the hackathon had been in person, intense, with that energy of a live event all around. In 2020, everything changed: calls, screens, remote communication, uncertainty, and the need to build trust quickly with people I was just meeting.

Even so, it turned out to be one of the best experiences I could have had at that moment.

## Solving problems with people you just met

In this challenge, I worked with Liggia Lima, Karla Pereira, and Josinaldo Júnior. We also had Henrique Gomes on the journey and important help from Leonardo Pinheiro at several points.

When you enter a hackathon with people you just met, the first part of the challenge isn't technical. It's human. You need to understand how each person thinks, what each one knows how to do, where each one can contribute, and how to make decisions without turning everything into an endless meeting.

It was beautiful to see it happen. Little by little, the team found its rhythm, divided responsibilities, and turned an idea into a solution.

## Open data, mobile, and API

The challenge involved creating a technological solution from open data. This kind of proposal forces the team to look at the problem first: what information exists, what pain does it help explain, and how do you turn that into a useful experience?

To build our solution, we used a straightforward stack:

- React Native for the mobile app.
- Express.js for the backend and API construction.

React Native helped create a mobile experience within the short marathon timeframe. Express.js allowed us to build a simple, fast-to-iterate API that was enough to validate the proposal. In hackathons, a good tool is one that helps the team go from zero without creating unnecessary complexity.

## CEIA/UFG and mentorship

During this journey, I also had the opportunity to get to know CEIA, the Center of Excellence in Artificial Intelligence at UFG (Federal University of Goiás). It was a very important contact because it broadened my perception of how data, research, and applied technology can connect with real-world challenges.

I'm also very grateful to the mentors who supported us. In particular, Leonardo Pinheiro helped us at several points. Hackathons often carry that image of a team solving everything on their own, but the truth is that good mentorship accelerates thinking, asks better questions, and helps pull the team out of dead ends.

## The result

We got 2nd place.

More than the placement, this hackathon marked me through collaboration. I met extraordinary people, excellent professionals, and once again realized that software is much more than writing code. It's communication, trust, listening, and the ability to build something together even when the conditions aren't ideal.

In the middle of a pandemic, managing to create, present, and take second place with a team formed almost entirely remotely was a huge achievement. It was also another step in building a path that, little by little, showed me that I enjoyed solving hard problems with good people around.
